Transversus‑first progression to a strong, pain‑smart back

Start with diaphragmatic breathing and TA activation. Progress through validated anti‑extension / anti‑rotation drills, then integrate hip‑hinge and loaded carries. Built‑in safety gates and calculators show when to advance—never guess.

This page is educational—not medical advice. If you have significant pain, new symptoms, or uncertainty, consult a licensed clinician first.

Safety first: red flags, pain “traffic light,” and load context

🚩 Red flags → seek emergency care now

  • New bowel/bladder changes (retention, incontinence), or saddle numbness.
  • Rapidly worsening leg weakness, bilateral sciatica, or numbness around the buttocks/genitals.

These may indicate cauda equina syndrome—a surgical emergency. See references [7–8].

🟢🟠🔴 Pain “traffic light”

Use symptoms to dose activity. If pain worsens, modify or stop.

  • 🟢 0–3/10: usually acceptable—continue.
  • 🟠 4–5/10: acceptable with caution—reduce volume/complexity.
  • 🔴 6–10/10: excessive—stop and regress.

NHS guidance on acceptable pain levels during back‑pain rehab; stop if pain worsens. See [9–10].

“2‑hour rule”

If pain persists > 2 hours after exercise, reduce reps/sets next time. See the NHS leaflet in [11].

📦 Lifting risk context (work/day‑to‑day)

Under ideal conditions, NIOSH’s recommended limit constant (RWL base) is 23 kg / ~51 lb. Real tasks often include multipliers that reduce safe capacity (reach, asymmetry, frequency, etc.). This is not a training target; use it to contextualize loads in later phases. See [12].

Program (5 phases) — from activation to high‑load capacity

Phase 0 — Breath & TA activation

Goal: Find the diaphragm. Selectively recruit TA without breath‑holding or pelvic tilt.

Diaphragmatic breathing High

  • How: One hand on chest, one below ribs; inhale through nose so belly rises; exhale through pursed lips letting abdomen fall. 5–10 minutes, 1–2x/day. [1–2]
  • Why: Calms the system and coordinates the diaphragm with core stabilizers.
  • Stop/regress: Dizziness, breath‑holding patterns—shorten sets.

Abdominal Drawing‑In Maneuver (ADIM) Medium

  • How: Supine 90/90 hips/knees; draw navel gently “up and in” (fingers just inside ASIS feel TA tension) while breathing normally; 5–10s holds × 10 reps. [3–6]
  • Why: Preferentially recruits TA; imaging studies validate training/assessment methods. [3–6]
  • Stop/regress: If you bear down, hold your breath, or tilt pelvis—reset and reduce hold time.
Phase 0 gate
  • Breath: 5 mins comfortable diaphragmatic breathing without neck/shoulder tension. Low
  • ADIM: 10×10s holds with normal breathing; palpable TA tension; no pelvic tilt. Low
  • Pain: ≤5/10 and returns to baseline within 2 hours. Medium

Phase 1 — Supine control & anti‑extension

Goal: Control pelvic tilt and trunk while moving arms/legs.

Posterior pelvic tilt (PPT) High

  • How: Supine, knees bent; gently flatten/lift lumbar spine (Mayo “lower back flexibility” guidance). 5–10 reps → 2–3 sets. [13]
  • Why: Teaches lumbopelvic control used in all later drills.

Supine march (TA set) High

  • How: TA brace + lift one foot to ~90°, alternate slowly for 45–60s. [14]
  • Why: Early dynamic control under light load.

Bridge High

Glute‑dominant support for the posterior chain; 8–12 reps × 2–3 sets. [13]

Dead bug (progressions) High

  • How: Arms up, knees over hips; alternate opposite arm/leg without lumbar arching. 5–12 reps/side × 2–3 sets. [15–16]
  • Stop/regress: If back lifts, shorten lever or do heel taps.
Phase 1 gate
  • PPT: 2×10 with controlled breath, no pain >5/10. Low
  • March: 60s steady without pelvic tilt. Low
  • Dead bug: 2×10/side with lumbar neutral. Low
  • Post‑session pain settles ≤2 hrs. Medium

Phase 2 — Anti‑extension & anti‑rotation endurance

Goal: Build isometric endurance & stiffness for daily tasks.

Front plank (forearms or high) High

Elbows under shoulders; glutes on; slight posterior pelvic tilt; build multiple short holds. [17]

Side plank (modified → full) High

From knees → feet; maintain straight line shoulder→heel. [18]

Bird‑dog High

Opposite arm/leg reach with minimal trunk shift; hold 3–5s. [18]

Pallof press (anti‑rotation) High

Cable/band at chest height; press out resisting rotation. 3×10–15 or 20–30s isos. [19]

Phase 2 gate (objective)

Use the McGill calculator in Tools. Targets below reflect ACE protocol sheets.

  • Ratios: Flexion:Extension < 1.0; Side‑bridge:Extension < 0.75; Right:Left within ±0.05. High [20–22]
  • Consistency: Can perform planks/side planks in sets of 10–30s with crisp form (multiple sets preferred over long single holds). Medium

Phase 3 — Integrated strength & loaded carries

Goal: Hinge, row, and carry with a braced, neutral spine.

Hip hinge patterning High

Dowel 3‑point contact; push hips back; maintain neutral spine. [23]

Bent‑over row (dumbbell) High

Row with spine neutral; scapular control; 8–12 reps × 2–4 sets. [24]

Farmer’s carry (bilateral) High

Tall posture, slow walk, no sway. 20–40 m bouts. [25]

Suitcase carry (unilateral) High

Anti‑lateral‑flexion challenge; switch sides. [26]

Phase 3 gate
  • Hinge & row: 2–3 sets with neutral spine maintained under moderate load. Medium
  • Carry test: 2×20–30 m farmer’s carries with posture intact at a load you can set down under control. If using ACE guidance, a long‑term goal is “total load ≈ bodyweight” (split across both hands) — advanced only. Medium [27]
  • Pain: ≤5/10, resolves ≤2 hrs. Medium

Phase 4 — Higher load & dynamic core

Goal: Work‑ready capacity with spine‑sparing mechanics.

Kettlebell deadlift (floor) High

Foot stance hip–shoulder width; hinge; grip; drive through floor; neutral neck. [28]

Stability‑ball rollout (knees) Medium

Forearms on ball, roll out without lumbar sag. 3×6–10. [29]

Hanging/knee‑raise (or Captain’s Chair) Medium

Progress from chair → hanging; avoid lumbar arch/hip‑flexor dominance. [30]

Phase 4 gate & work simulation
  • Deadlift: 3×5 with perfect hinge and no pain escalation (choose load you can pause at mid‑shin with control). Medium
  • Carry & row volumes match your job demands using posture‑first rules. For context on safe lifting, remember the NIOSH 23 kg (51 lb) ideal‑condition constant—real‑world limits are often lower due to reach, twist, frequency. High [12]
